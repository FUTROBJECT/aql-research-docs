#20 — Sunny Chatbot – Student Emotional Support CC: Adaptable

Validated   Category: Student Support Services  |  Institution: Arizona State University  |  Tool: Conversational AI chatbot

Description: AI chatbot designed to provide emotional support and help students adjust to college life; part of ASU’s broader OpenAI partnership.

Navigator Mapping

Primary: SS-04 (Student-Facing Chatbot) Tier 1
With tier override to: Tier 2 or Tier 3 depending on whether it collects mental health disclosures

Why this mapping: At surface level this is a student-facing chatbot (SS-04). However, the emotional support function means it likely processes sensitive mental health data. SS-04’s screening questions would need to determine: Does this chatbot collect or respond to emotional/mental health disclosures? If yes, the risk tier escalates.

Coverage: Partial — emotional support gap. SS-04 covers informational chatbots. Neither SS-04 nor SS-07 addresses AI-delivered emotional support as a distinct function.

Key governance: P5 (harm from bad crisis advice), P6 (emotional disclosures as sensitive data), P7 (student knows it’s AI?), NG-06.

Navigator v2 Framework Reference

Quick reference to the Navigator v2 framework components referenced throughout both crosswalks.

Nine Governance Pillars

Each pillar has assessment questions, a 3-point scoring rubric, and remediation actions.

P1 — Purpose & Educational Legitimacy

“If it doesn’t serve students, it doesn’t belong here.”

Every AI deployment must serve a genuine educational purpose.

P2 — Equity & Differential Impact

“If it doesn’t work for all your students, it doesn’t work.”

Account for differential impacts on diverse CC populations. Disaggregated performance data required.

P3 — Human Authority & Decision Accountability

“Who is accountable when the AI is wrong?”

Humans make consequential decisions. Named reviewer before AI affects students.

P4 — Student & Faculty Agency

“Your students are adults. Your faculty run the classroom. Respect both.”

Opt-out, configuration, involvement in procurement. Adjuncts included.

P5 — Risk Proportionality & Harm Mitigation

“What’s the worst that happens to a student if this tool fails?”

Failure mode determines governance requirements.

P6 — Data Stewardship & System Integrity

“Your data, your rules. Not the vendor’s.”

FERPA compliance, data ownership, vendor training opt-out, portability.

Community College Adaptability Analysis

Key finding: Zero of the 38 SS-relevant use cases across both sources originated from a community college. All come from R1/R2 universities, specialized institutions, or vendors. CCs are invisible in the current AI use case literature.

1. Rating Methodology

Each of the 38 SS-relevant use cases was assessed against CC-specific constraints:

  • IT capacity: Small teams (often 1–3 people), no dedicated AI/ML staff
  • Staffing: Heavy adjunct reliance, high counselor-to-student ratios
  • Student population: Part-time, working, first-gen, multilingual, stop-out patterns
  • Budget: Lower per-student funding than R1 institutions
  • Mandates: Guided pathways, AB 1705, Vision for Success metrics
  • Infrastructure: Shared SIS (PeopleSoft/Banner), Canvas, CCCApply
