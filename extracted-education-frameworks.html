Key Principles

Eight foundational pillars: Strategy, Governance, Data Privacy and Security, Procurement, Implementation, Competency Development, Risk Management, and Utility and Effectiveness. Mandates AI Governance Committees, human verification of AI outputs, NIST-based risk assessment, and explicit prohibition on using school data to train AI models.

Target Audience: Local Education Agencies (LEAs) in Alabama; adaptable template for district-level policy

  • Eight-pillar structure provides complete governance framework

3. Responsible AI and Tech Justice: A Guide for K-12 Education
Organization: Kapor Foundation / Kapor Center Year: 2024 Toolkit K-12 Ethics Literacy Document Link Downnload Link

Key Principles

Six inquiry-based components centering racial and social justice: (1) Examine AI creation ecosystem, (2) Interrogate technology-human relationships, (3) Explore societal impacts, (4) Interrogate personal AI usage, (5) Build critical lens on data collection, usage, and reporting, (6) Minimize/eliminate AI-caused harm. Explicitly centers how AI disproportionately impacts marginalized communities.

6. The Ethical Framework for AI in Education
Organization: Institute for Ethical AI in Education (University of Buckingham) Year: 2021 Framework Cross-sector Governance Ethics Procurement Document LinkDownload PDF

Key Principles

Nine requirements for ethical AI in education:

  • (1) Achieving Educational Goals — AI used for well-defined goals with strong evidence
  • (2) Forms of Assessment — recognize broader aptitudes including metacognition
  • (3) Administration & Workload — boost institutional capacity while respecting human relationships
  • (4) Equity — promote equity between learner groups
  • (5) Learner Autonomy — increase learner control over development
  • (6) Privacy — balance privacy with legitimate data use
  • (7) Transparency & Accountability — humans accountable for AI decisions
  • (8) Informed Participation — stakeholders understand and consent to AI use
  • (9) Ethical Design — resources designed with learners' interests at heart

Includes procurement checklist for evaluating AI vendors.

Target Audience: Education leaders and practitioners making procurement and application decisions; AI suppliers

CC Applicability: High

Strengths for CC:

  • Procurement-focused — directly usable for CC technology evaluation and RFPs
  • Nine-requirement structure provides systematic governance foundation
  • Sector-agnostic design — explicitly for 'any educational setting'
  • Checklist format (pages 4–9) immediately actionable

Gaps for CC:

  • No academic integrity or AI detection component
  • Equity framing less robust than justice-centered frameworks (e.g., Kapor)
  • UK-based policy references may need localization
  • Institute defunct since 2021 — no ongoing updates or support
  • No workforce development or CTE considerations

Adaptation Notes: Only procurement-focused framework in inventory. The nine requirements provide foundational governance architecture that Khan Academy and others have built upon. Most valuable for CC technology selection committees evaluating AI tutoring, adaptive learning, and assessment tools. Pre-dates generative AI concerns, so should be combined with more recent frameworks (TeachAI Toolkit, Alabama Template) for comprehensive coverage. The procurement checklist can be adapted directly into CC vendor evaluation rubrics.

12. 7 Principles for Responsible AI in Education
Organization: TeachAI (Code.org, ETS, ISTE, Khan Academy, World Economic Forum) Year: 2024 Policy General Ethics Governance Privacy Literacy Document LinkDownload link

Key Principles

Seven principles: (1) Purpose: Connect AI to educational goals, (2) Compliance: Affirm adherence to existing policies, (3) Knowledge: Promote AI literacy, (4) Balance: Realize benefits and address risks, (5) Integrity: Advance academic integrity, (6) Agency: Maintain human decision-making, (7) Evaluation: Continuously assess AI impact.

Three levels of AI use for assignments:

  • Permissive: Students can freely use AI for ideas, proofreading, organizing
  • Moderate: AI for brainstorming/research only; core content must be original
  • Restrictive: AI prohibited; all work must be student's original creation

Target Audience: Education leaders, policymakers, school administrators globally

14. MagicSchool's Responsible AI Framework / MagicSchool AI Safety and Privacy Documentation
Organization: MagicSchool Year: 2024 Platform K-12 Privacy Governance Document LinkDownload Source

Key Principles

Responsible AI for education with built-in safeguards. SOC 2 certified, FERPA/COPPA compliant. Explicit commitment: 'We don't use student or teacher data to train AI.' Advanced data dashboards for district monitoring, student learning insights, AI instructional coach for teachers.

Four Pillars:

  • Built-in safeguards (clear terms, on-screen guidance, bias warnings, professional learning)
  • Designed for equity (human/automated bias monitoring, real-time quality control, friction where it matters)
  • Multi-model infrastructure (not locked to one provider, can route to best models)
  • Teachers at the center (opt-in tools, educators set context, amplify not automate)
