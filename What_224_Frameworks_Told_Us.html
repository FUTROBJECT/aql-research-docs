How the Research Worked

We scanned 224 AI governance frameworks across 12 global domains — from the African Union’s Continental AI Strategy to Singapore’s Agentic AI Addendum to the National Association of Insurance Commissioners’ model bulletin. From that scan, we identified 62 high-priority candidates, then deeply analyzed 14 frameworks that had the most to offer community colleges. Every recommendation below traces back to specific provisions in those 14 frameworks, cross-referenced against each other and tested for CC feasibility.

This research practiced what we preach. Using the AI Learning Mode framework:
  • EXPLORE mode — AI-assisted discovery across all 12 global domains, identifying 224 candidate frameworks. AI did the scanning; I directed where to look and what to flag.
  • ASSISTED mode — AI-assisted extraction and structuring of 14 priority frameworks, with explicit tagging of what AI retrieved versus what I evaluated for CC relevance.
  • RESTRICTED/HUMAN-ONLY — All prioritization decisions, gap identification, conflict resolution, and provision development. The judgment calls — what matters for community colleges, what to adopt vs. reject, where the real gaps are — are mine.
This is exactly the kind of transparent AI collaboration our framework recommends. The full research corpus is available if you want to dig into any specific claim. This document gives you the conclusions and tells you where to look if you want the evidence.

6 Findings That Should Change How We Build This

1 Community Colleges Are Invisible

Of the 14 priority frameworks we analyzed, only 2 address community colleges at all (ASCCC and ATD). Federal frameworks like OMB M-25-21 mention “education” but mean research universities. International frameworks assume institutional resources CCs don’t have. This isn’t a gap we’re filling — it’s an entire market that doesn’t exist yet.

We are not competing with existing CC governance frameworks. There aren’t any.
Dig deeper: discovery-summary.md, project-final-summary.md (Finding 1)

2 Six Problems No Framework on Earth Addresses

We mapped all 14 frameworks against 9 CC-specific governance needs. Six of those nine have zero coverage from any framework globally: adult learner contexts, dual enrollment, non-credit programs, adjunct faculty provisions, transfer student AI, and accessibility/disability. We developed 42 novel provisions to fill these gaps. This is where our genuine novelty lives — not in scanning 224 frameworks, but in building what none of them built.

Dig deeper: gap-coverage-matrix.md, cc-provision-mapping.md

3 Adjunct Faculty Are the Binding Constraint

This came through in framework after framework, by absence: no one accounts for the fact that 70%+ of CC instruction is delivered by adjunct faculty who lack equitable access to tools, training, and governance participation. Any AI framework that ignores adjunct realities will fail at the implementation stage. Our framework must include equitable AI tool access, compensated training, and governance voice for adjuncts.

This is non-negotiable. A governance framework that doesn’t account for adjunct faculty realities is a governance framework that doesn’t work at community colleges.
Dig deeper: best-fit-elements.md (Gap 5), aql-development-recommendations.md

4 Detection vs. Documentation: The Research Is Clear

This is the most contentious question in educational AI governance, and the global evidence strongly favors documentation-forward approaches over AI detection tools. The reasons: 15–30% false positive rates in detection tools, disproportionate impact on ESL students and students with disabilities, and the workforce preparation value of teaching students to document their AI use transparently.

Recommendation: A documentation-forward position with detection tools informing (but never determining) academic integrity decisions.
Dig deeper: aql-development-recommendations.md (Risk Considerations), analysis-summary.md

5 The Insurance Industry Has the Best Operational Model

This was the biggest surprise. The National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) model bulletin provides the clearest operational template for what a “written AI governance program” should look like — documentation standards, cross-functional governance committees, consumer/student notification requirements, third-party vendor accountability, and audit rights. We adapted their lifecycle model (policy → underwriting → claims → customer service) to education (enrollment → advising → assessment → completion). Singapore’s framework gave us the best model for agentic AI governance — autonomy limits, human checkpoints, escalation requirements.

Dig deeper: best-fit-elements.md (Framework 13: NAIC, Framework 7: Singapore)

6 Agentic AI Governance Is Urgent — and Almost Nobody Has It

Tutoring agents and advising bots are already deploying in colleges. Only Singapore’s Agentic AI Addendum addresses governance for autonomous AI systems. We need provisions for autonomy limits by decision type, human accountability checkpoints, escalation requirements, and accuracy standards — and we need them before these systems proliferate further.

This is a “build it now or play catch-up for years” situation.
Dig deeper: analysis-summary.md (Gap 6), best-fit-elements.md (Singapore framework)

5 Decisions the Research Supports

These aren’t open questions — the research points clearly in one direction for each.

Decision Recommendation Confidence Why
Detection or documentation? Documentation-forward High Equity evidence, false positive rates, workforce value
Dedicated Chief AI Officer? No — use existing committee structure High Budget-unrealistic; adapted OMB board model works
Which framework foundation? ASCCC 5 principles + ATD “AI Agility” + GAO 4 principles High CC-developed, equity-centered, structurally sound
Vendor contract language? Adopt NAIC audit rights + IEEE 7004 RFP language High Immediately usable, tested in regulated industries
Tiered or one-size-fits-all? Tiered (3 levels based on institutional maturity) High EDUCAUSE model adapted for CC resource variation

The 30-Second Version

Community colleges are invisible in global AI governance. Nobody has built what we’re building.

The 42 novel provisions we developed aren’t opinions — they’re grounded in cross-sector analysis of what works.
