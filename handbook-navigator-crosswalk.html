3. Detailed Crosswalk — Student Services–Relevant Use Cases

Each handbook use case is identified by its exact row number (#) from the "Use Cases" sheet, its category, its status (Validated or Proposed), and mapped to the Navigator v2 framework with specificity.

#20 — Sunny Chatbot – Student Emotional Support

Validated   Category: Student Support Services  |  Institution: Arizona State University  |  Tool: Conversational AI chatbot

Description: AI chatbot designed to provide emotional support and help students adjust to college life; part of ASU’s broader OpenAI partnership.

Navigator Mapping

Primary: SS-04 (Student-Facing Chatbot) Tier 1
With tier override to: Tier 2 or Tier 3 depending on whether it collects mental health disclosures

Why this mapping: At surface level this is a student-facing chatbot (SS-04). However, the emotional support function means it likely processes sensitive mental health data, which the Navigator’s tier override mechanism should catch. SS-04’s screening questions would need to determine: Does this chatbot collect or respond to emotional/mental health disclosures? If yes, the risk tier escalates and additional pillars activate.

Coverage assessment: Partial — emotional support gap. SS-04 covers informational chatbots. SS-07 (Basic Needs) covers resource referrals. Neither addresses AI-delivered emotional support as a distinct function. This is an ambiguous middle ground.

Key governance considerations:

  • P5 (Risk Proportionality): What happens if the chatbot gives harmful advice to a student in crisis?
  • P6 (Data Stewardship): Emotional disclosures are sensitive data. FERPA intersects with potential HIPAA considerations.
  • P7 (Transparency): Does the student know they are talking to an AI about their mental health?
  • NG-06: Student disclosure of AI use is directly relevant.

#21 — Pounce Chatbot – Enrollment & Persistence

Validated   Category: Student Support Services  |  Institution: Georgia State University  |  Tool: Pounce conversational AI

Description: Reduces friction in student support and improves persistence through AI-driven enrollment nudges, advising triage, and proactive outreach.

Navigator Mapping

This is a multi-function tool. It maps to multiple Navigator use cases simultaneously:

  • SS-04 (Student-Facing Chatbot) Tier 1 — the chatbot interface itself
  • SS-10 (Registration & Enrollment) Tier 2 — enrollment nudges
  • SS-01 (AI Advising / Guided Pathways) Tier 3 — advising triage
  • SS-02 (Early Alert) Tier 3 — proactive outreach / flagging

Why this mapping: Pounce is described as doing three distinct things: enrollment nudges (SS-10), advising triage (SS-01), and proactive outreach (SS-02-adjacent). Each function carries a different risk tier. The Navigator’s single-use-case-at-a-time design means Pounce would need to be assessed multiple times for its different functions — which is the correct governance approach.

Coverage assessment: Covered across multiple use cases, but reveals a gap. The Navigator does not yet have guidance for evaluating multi-function platforms that span multiple use cases. The highest-risk function (advising triage, Tier 3) should set the governance floor.

Key governance considerations:

  • P2 (Equity): Enrollment nudges may have differential impact by demographics.
  • P3 (Human Authority): Is a counselor reviewing advising triage recommendations?
  • P7 (Contestability): Can a student contest being flagged or not flagged for outreach?
  • NG-03: No human override for consequential decisions — does a human review before the nudge?

#22 — WoeBot – AI Mental Health Support

Validated   Category: Student Support Services  |  Institution: Stanford University  |  Tool: CBT-based conversational AI

Description: AI chatbot using cognitive behavioral therapy techniques; 8 randomized control trials completed showing significant reduction in depression symptoms over 2 weeks.

Navigator Mapping

Primary: SS-XX (Custom) — no existing Navigator use case covers AI-delivered therapeutic interventions
Recommended tier: Tier 3 (High Risk)

Why this mapping: WoeBot goes well beyond a chatbot (SS-04) or basic needs referral (SS-07). It delivers CBT-based therapy — a clinical intervention. No Navigator use case addresses this. Closest is SS-07 (Basic Needs Support) for resource connection, but WoeBot is not connecting students to resources; it is the resource delivering the intervention.

Coverage assessment: GAP. No mental health/counseling AI use case exists.

Key governance considerations:

  • P3 (Human Authority): Who is clinically responsible if the AI mishandles a crisis? Is there a licensed counselor overseeing the tool?
  • P5 (Risk Proportionality): Worst case: a student in active crisis receiving harmful guidance.
  • P6 (Data Stewardship): Mental health data may fall under HIPAA in addition to FERPA.
  • P7 (Transparency): Does the student understand the limitations of AI-delivered therapy?
  • NG-03: No human override for consequential decisions is directly relevant.
  • NG-06: Student disclosure of AI use is critical — students must know it’s AI.

#23 — AI Chatbot for Student Mental Wellness

Validated   Category: Student Support Services  |  Institution: University of Memphis  |  Tool: Wysa, Woebot, Tess

Description: Digital mental health intervention helping college students manage anxiety and depression; open trial results published showing efficacy.

Navigator Mapping

Primary: SS-XX (Custom) — same gap as #22
Recommended tier: Tier 3 (High Risk)

Why this mapping: Same rationale as #22 (WoeBot). This use case reinforces the gap — multiple institutions are deploying AI mental health tools, and the Navigator has no dedicated use case for this category.

Coverage assessment: GAP. Same as #22.

Additional governance note: These are third-party tools (Wysa, Woebot, Tess) — P8 (Vendor Accountability) is critical. Are these vendors sharing student mental health data? NG-02 (students are not training data) must be checked: are these vendors training models on student therapy conversations?

#24 — University of Hawaii AI Chatbots (10 Campuses)

Validated   Category: Student Support Services  |  Institution: University of Hawaii System  |  Tool: AI chatbot platform

Description: AI chatbots across all 10 campuses: 94% student opt-in rate; 4,294 questions answered without human intervention Aug–Dec 2025; saved 357 staff hours; flagged 2,533 students for follow-up.

Navigator Mapping

Another multi-function tool:

  • SS-04 (Student-Facing Chatbot) Tier 1 — the FAQ/question-answering function (4,294 questions answered)
  • SS-02 (Early Alert / Student Risk System) Tier 3 — the flagging function (2,533 students flagged for follow-up)

Why this mapping: The chatbot function is straightforward SS-04. But the stat “flagged 2,533 students for follow-up” is an early alert function — the tool is making risk determinations about students. That pushes the flagging component to Tier 3.

Coverage assessment: Covered across SS-04 + SS-02. Multi-function guidance still needed.

Key governance considerations:

  • P2 (Equity): 94% opt-in is high, but what about the 6% who did not? Are they disproportionately from specific demographics?
  • P6 (Data Stewardship): 4,294 questions answered — where does that data live? Who controls it?
  • P7 (Transparency): When a student is “flagged for follow-up,” do they know?
  • NG-05: FERPA-compliant data sharing agreement — 10-campus system with external vendor.
  • NG-07: Decisions without recourse — applies to the flagging function.

#25 — Indiana University ChatGPT Edu Rollout

Validated   Category: Student Support Services  |  Institution: Indiana University  |  Tool: ChatGPT Edu

Description: Second-largest ChatGPT Edu rollout globally; 120,000 students, faculty, and staff get access; complements institution-wide GenAI 101 course.

Navigator Mapping

Primary: SS-04 (Student-Facing Chatbot) Tier 1 for the student support function
Secondary: SS-05 (AI Course Tutoring Assistant) Tier 2 for instructional use

Why this mapping: This is a campus-wide ChatGPT Edu deployment — a general-purpose AI tool, not a Student Services-specific tool. It maps to SS-04 when students use it for service navigation and SS-05 when used as a tutoring assistant. Unlike Pounce or Hawaii’s chatbot, it does not appear to make decisions about students (no flagging, no advising triage).

Coverage assessment: Covered. Lower governance intensity needed.

Key governance considerations:

  • P1 (Purpose): Is the educational purpose clearly defined, or is this “give everyone ChatGPT”?
  • P8 (Vendor Accountability): OpenAI as vendor — data handling, training data policies, contract terms at 120K-user scale.

#26 — Penn State MyResource – AI Advising

Validated   Category: Student Support Services  |  Institution: Penn State University  |  Tool: MyResource AI platform

Description: Helps students navigate advising and services 24/7; governed agent reducing bottlenecks in enrollment, learning support, and operational coordination.

Navigator Mapping

Primary: SS-01 (AI Advising / Guided Pathways) Tier 3

Why this mapping: This is a near-exact match for SS-01. The tool provides 24/7 advising navigation — a core guided pathways function. Penn State describes it as a “governed agent,” which aligns with the Navigator’s P3 (Human Authority) pillar.

Coverage assessment: Fully covered. The Navigator’s SS-01 screening questions, P1/P2/P3/P5 pillar focus, and equity flag (“errors compound for first-gen and Pell-eligible”) directly apply.

Key governance considerations:

  • P3 (Human Authority): Penn State calls this a “governed agent” — what does that mean operationally? Who reviews its recommendations?
  • P5 (Risk Proportionality): 24/7 availability means bad advice can happen at 2 AM with no human backup.
  • P2 (Equity): Navigator’s equity flag directly applies — first-gen students may over-rely on AI advising without calibrating it against human counselor input.

#30 — Student Dropout Prediction via Card Swipe Data

Validated   Category: Administrative Operations  |  Institution: University of Arizona  |  Tool: ML, network science, big data analytics

Description: ML analysis of student ID card swipes for dropout prediction; 73% accuracy from first day, improving over time using social network and engagement patterns.

Navigator Mapping

Primary: SS-02 (Early Alert / Student Risk System) Tier 3
Secondary: SS-09 (Student Journey Mapping) Tier 2

Why this mapping: This is an early alert / risk prediction system. The data source (card swipes = physical location tracking) adds a surveillance dimension that the Navigator’s SS-02 equity flag directly addresses: “Risk profiling creates surveillance dynamic. Disparate impact on students of color.”

Coverage assessment: Fully covered. SS-02 was designed for exactly this type of system.

Key governance considerations:

  • P6 (Data Stewardship): Using physical location tracking for risk profiling is a significant privacy concern. Card swipe patterns reveal daily routines, social networks, and physical movements.
  • P7 (Transparency): Do students know their card swipes are being analyzed for dropout prediction?
  • P2 (Equity): Card swipe patterns correlate with commuter status, work schedules, and caregiving responsibilities — all disproportionately affecting CC students. 73% accuracy means 27% error rate — disaggregated by demographics?
  • NG-04: Can the institution independently audit this model for bias?

#32 — Enrollment Yield Prediction

Validated   Category: Administrative Operations  |  Institution: Multiple universities  |  Tool: Othot, Element451

Description: AI analyzes historical enrollment patterns to optimize financial aid distribution and maximize enrollment yield from admitted students.

Navigator Mapping

Primary: SS-10 (Registration & Enrollment) Tier 2
Secondary: SS-03 (Financial Aid Triage) Tier 3 — because it optimizes financial aid distribution

Why this mapping: The enrollment prediction function maps to SS-10. But the “optimize financial aid distribution” component is an SS-03 function — AI is influencing how much aid students receive based on predicted enrollment likelihood. This is a consequential financial decision.

Coverage assessment: Partial — yield optimization gap. SS-10 covers enrollment decisions. SS-03 covers financial aid triage. But the specific practice of using AI to determine aid amounts based on predicted enrollment probability falls between them. A student predicted to enroll regardless may receive less aid — a systematic disadvantage for students with fewer alternatives (often low-income, first-gen).

Key governance considerations:

  • P2 (Equity): Yield prediction models may systematically disadvantage students who are less likely to enroll regardless of aid — often the most vulnerable.
  • P3 (Human Authority): Who reviews the AI’s aid allocation recommendations before they reach students?
  • NG-03: No human override for consequential decisions.

Navigator v2 Framework Reference

This tab provides a quick reference to the Navigator v2 framework components referenced throughout the crosswalk. Use this to orient to the pillars, risk tiers, no-go criteria, and use cases before reviewing the detailed mappings.

Nine Governance Pillars

Each pillar has assessment questions, a 3-point scoring rubric (3 = Good, 2 = Partial, 1 = Poor), and remediation actions. Together they form the evaluation engine.

P1 — Purpose & Educational Legitimacy

“If it doesn’t serve students, it doesn’t belong here.”

Every AI deployment must serve a genuine educational purpose and protect the meaning of credentials. This pillar asks: Can you articulate why this tool exists in Student Services? Is the educational benefit clear and measurable, or is this technology for technology’s sake?

Questions: 5  |  HUMANS mapping: AQL-original

P2 — Equity & Differential Impact

“If it doesn’t work for all your students, it doesn’t work.”

AI governance must center equity and account for differential impacts on diverse CC student populations. This pillar asks: Has the tool been tested with the populations your college actually serves? Do you have disaggregated performance data across Pell-eligible, first-gen, multilingual, and racially diverse students?

Questions: 7  |  HUMANS mapping: U, A

P3 — Human Authority & Decision Accountability

“Who is accountable when the AI is wrong?”

Humans — not algorithms — make consequential decisions about students. This pillar asks: Is there a named human who reviews AI outputs before they affect a student’s enrollment, financial aid, or academic standing? What happens when the AI is wrong at 2 AM?

P4 — Student & Faculty Agency

“Your students are adults. Your faculty run the classroom. Respect both.”

Students and faculty are empowered participants, not subjects. This pillar asks: Can students opt out? Can faculty configure the tool for their course? Were students and faculty involved in the procurement decision? Are adjuncts included in training?

Questions: 8  |  HUMANS mapping: H, U

P5 — Risk Proportionality & Harm Mitigation

“What’s the worst that happens to a student if this tool fails?”

Governance intensity matches risk level. This pillar asks: What is the failure mode? If the tool gives wrong advice, does a student miss a deadline, take the wrong class, or lose financial aid? The answer determines governance requirements.

Questions: 6  |  HUMANS mapping: S

P6 — Data Stewardship & System Integrity

“Your data, your rules. Not the vendor’s.”

Student data is a public trust. This pillar asks: Where does student data go? Who owns it? Is the data sharing agreement FERPA-compliant? Can the vendor use student data to train models? Can you get your data back if you leave?

Questions: 8  |  HUMANS mapping: M

P7 — Transparency & Contestability

“Decisions without recourse are not governance.”

Students and faculty have the right to know when AI influences decisions that affect them — and the right to contest those decisions. This pillar asks: Does the student know AI was involved? Can they challenge the outcome? Is the decision-making process explainable?

Questions: 7  |  HUMANS mapping: N

P8 — Vendor Accountability & Institutional Sovereignty

“You’re signing the contract. Make sure it protects your students, not just the vendor.”

Community colleges are buyers, not builders. Vendor relationships must protect institutional sovereignty. This pillar asks: Does the contract allow independent bias audits? Can you exit without losing data? Are switching costs punitive? Does the vendor’s business model depend on your students’ data?

Questions: 7  |  HUMANS mapping: S

P9 — Institutional Capacity & Continuous Governance

“Don’t add tools to an ungoverned environment. Build the floor first.”

Governance must be achievable at every institutional scale. This pillar asks: Does someone own this tool’s governance? Is there a review cadence? Is there budget for training? This pillar is about the institution, not the tool — and it fills a gap the CCCCO HUMANS framework does not address.

Risk Tier System

Tier Risk Level When It Applies Governance Requirements
Tier 1 Lower Risk Informational only. No decisions affecting access. No sensitive data. (e.g., FAQ chatbot, career exploration tool) Score P1, P2, P6, P7, P8 minimum. Others recommended.
Tier 2 Medium Risk Touches advising or instructional core. Human reviews outputs. Accesses student records. (e.g., tutoring assistant, multilingual support) All 9 pillars scored. P2 and P6 must score ≥ 2.
Tier 3 High Risk Affects enrollment, financial aid, academic standing. Automated or semi-automated decisions. Sensitive data. (e.g., early alert system, AI advising) All 9 pillars mandatory. P2 and P6 ≥ 2. Full governance review required.
Override mechanism: Each use case has a default risk tier. Users can override (e.g., a chatbot that accesses student records upgrades from Tier 1 to Tier 2). Lowering the tier requires a documented reason.

Eight No-Go Criteria

Hard stops. If any condition is true, the tool should not deploy until resolved. Each has both a checklist detection path and a score-derived detection path.

NG-01: No clear educational purpose.
If you can’t articulate the educational purpose, don’t buy it.
Detection: P1 score = 1, OR screening indicates no educational purpose.
NG-02: Students are not training data.
Vendors that train models on student data are extracting value from the most vulnerable populations in higher education.
Detection: Explicit yes/no in no-go checklist.
NG-03: No human override for consequential decisions.
When AI makes decisions about financial aid, enrollment, or academic standing without human review, students lose recourse.
Detection: P3 score = 1 AND risk tier = High.
NG-04: Vendor blocks independent bias audits.
Vendors who block audits behind IP claims are choosing their property over your students’ civil rights.
Detection: Explicit yes/no in no-go checklist.
NG-05: No FERPA-compliant data sharing agreement.
This is a legal requirement, not a best practice.
Detection: Explicit yes/no in no-go checklist.
NG-06: No student disclosure of AI use.
Students have a right to know when AI influences decisions that affect them. Period.
Detection: P7 score = 1 OR explicit no-go question.
NG-07: Decisions without recourse are not governance.
If a student cannot contest an AI-influenced decision, you have automation, not governance.
Detection: P7 score = 1 OR explicit no-go question.
NG-08: Vendor lock-in undermines institutional sovereignty.
If you can’t leave without losing your students’ data or paying punitive switching costs, the vendor is governing you.
Detection: Explicit yes/no in no-go checklist.

Three-Layer Architecture

Layer Scope Contents
Layer 1 Portable Four Ethical Criteria: (1) Equitable Student Outcomes, (2) Quality Data Governed Responsibly, (3) Ethics & Safety Foundation, (4) Thoughtful Multi-Year Approach
Layer 2 Portable Nine Operational Pillars (P1–P9): The evaluation structure. Each pillar has questions, a 3-point scoring rubric, and remediation actions. This is the assessment engine.
Layer 3 California Only CCCCO HUMANS Framework: Binding for all 116 CCC colleges. The Navigator maps pillars to HUMANS letters. Non-California colleges skip this and still have a complete framework.
Portability: Layers 1 and 2 work for any community college system. Layer 3 adds California-specific CCCCO HUMANS alignment. A non-CA college using the Navigator sees the same pillars, same rubrics, same roadmap — minus the HUMANS column.
