The Core Idea

Navigate AI governance.
Built for Student Services.

Of 224 AI governance frameworks reviewed across 12 global domains, only 2 directly address community colleges. Community colleges serve nearly half of all US undergraduates, disproportionately serve historically marginalized populations, and are the primary workforce training pipeline. And increasingly, they are deploying AI tools in Student Services that make or influence decisions about students — advising, financial aid, early alerts, enrollment, tutoring — with no governance framework designed for their context.

The Navigator v2 answers that gap. Not with a document. With a tool that walks a college through evaluating a specific Student Services AI use case, scores it against nine governance pillars, and generates a tailored roadmap.

The product concept: An interactive web-based tool where institutional leadership identifies a Student Services AI use case, evaluates it against governance pillars, checks it against hard-stop criteria, and receives a generated governance roadmap — tailored to their institution’s archetype, risk tier, and scores.

Evolution from v1

The v1 Navigator was an institutional readiness assessment: “Tell us about your college, we’ll show you where to start.” The v2 Navigator is a tool-specific governance evaluation: “Tell us about the AI tool you’re considering, we’ll tell you if it’s ready for your students.”

Why Student Services

The scope decision was deliberate. Not all-of-institution. Not instruction. Not HR or finance. Student Services, specifically.

The reason: AI tools in Student Services make or influence decisions about students. Advising pathways. Financial aid triage. Early alert risk profiles. Enrollment recommendations. These are consequential decisions with real impact on access, equity, and outcomes — and they are the AI deployments spreading fastest through community colleges.

The specific risk: A CC purchasing an AI advising tool is not the same as a CC adopting a plagiarism detector or a faculty chatbot. Student Services AI can affect enrollment, financial aid, academic standing — the highest-stakes decisions a college makes about its students. That’s where governance matters most.

This scoping choice also makes the tool actionable. A VPSS or Dean can use this for a specific procurement decision. An AI governance committee can evaluate a specific tool. The output is a governance roadmap for that tool, not a general readiness report.

Why a Tool, Not a Document

A Static Document Says The Navigator Says
“Here are 9 governance pillars” “For this advising tool at your risk tier, score these 9 pillars using these rubrics”
“Consider equity implications” “Has the vendor provided disaggregated accuracy data across Pell, first-gen, and multilingual students?”
“Risk varies by use case” “Early Alert is Tier 3 (High Risk). Here are the 5 screening questions for that use case. Your answer triggered a PAUSE.”
“Human oversight is important” “P3 scored 1. No-Go: No human override for consequential decisions. Here’s what to do before deployment.”
“Implementation varies by institution” “You’re a Building institution evaluating a Tier 2 tool. Here’s your RACI, 90-day metrics, and review cadence.”
“Follow these principles” “Your scores show strong P6 & P8 but weak P4 & P7. That means: vendor is technically sound but product wasn’t designed with students as participants. You need to build the student-facing layer.”
The difference: A document presents principles. The Navigator applies them to a specific tool at a specific institution and produces specific actions. The framework architecture — pillars, tiers, archetypes, use cases — contains the logic for personalization. The tool exposes that logic to the user.

What Makes This Novel

No AI governance framework globally offers an interactive tool that evaluates specific use cases against scored pillars and generates tailored roadmaps. All 224 frameworks studied are documents. None scope to Student Services. None have use-case-specific screening. None have no-go criteria. The Navigator v2 is the first framework that governs tools, not just institutions.

Three-Layer Model

The framework uses a three-layer architecture. Layers 1 and 2 are portable (any CC system). Layer 3 is California-specific and optional.

Layer 1 — Portable
Four Ethical Criteria
1. Equitable Student Outcomes  •  2. Quality Data, Governed Responsibly  •  3. Ethics & Safety Foundation  •  4. Thoughtful, Multi-Year Approach
Layer 2 — Portable
Nine Operational Pillars
P1–P9: The evaluation structure. Each pillar has questions, a 3-point scoring rubric, and remediation actions. This is the assessment engine.
Layer 3 — California Only (Optional)
CCCCO HUMANS Framework
H–U–M–A–N–S is binding for all 116 CCC colleges. The Navigator maps pillars to HUMANS letters. Non-California colleges skip this and still have a complete framework.
Design principle: The California toggle adds HUMANS mapping to pillar scores and roadmap sections. It does not change what is assessed. A non-CA college evaluating the same tool sees the same pillars, the same rubrics, and gets the same governance roadmap — minus the HUMANS alignment column.
Gap identified: The CCCCO HUMANS framework does not address institutional capacity building (Criterion 4 / Pillar 9). The Navigator fills this gap. This is explicitly documented in the tool.

Three Archetypes

Lightweight
Start small. Start now.
Small or rural. Limited IT. 1–2 people wearing many hats. Under 5,000 students. No formal AI governance yet. Start with one Tier 1 or 2 use case, use templates directly, quarterly review.
Building
Structure enables innovation.
Mid-size with some analytics capacity. IT director and IR. AI committee forming. 2–3 use cases across tiers, cross-functional working group, begin disaggregated tracking. All 9 pillars active.
Scaling
Govern the portfolio, not just the tool.
Large with dedicated staff. Data warehouse, analytics team, innovation office. Multiple AI tools deployed. Portfolio approach, formal governance with project owners, bias audits for Tier 3.

Archetypes determine the roadmap’s RACI structure, metric complexity, and governance intensity. A Lightweight institution evaluating a Tier 1 chatbot gets a 2-page roadmap. A Scaling institution evaluating a Tier 3 early alert system gets 6 pages.

Step 0: Routing Questionnaire

Not every user arrives knowing which AI tool to evaluate. A VPSS or dean may say: “We want to do something with AI in Student Services but don’t know where to start.” Step 0 answers that question. Eight questions identify the institution’s context and route them to 2–3 recommended use cases, ranked by fit.

Position in the flow: Step 0 precedes the Use Case Selector (Step 1), Screening (Step 2), and Scoring (Step 3). Users who already know what they’re evaluating skip Step 0 entirely. Users who need help figuring out where to start begin here. The “Evaluate This Use Case →” button pre-selects the use case and carries archetype and institutional profile into the assessment.

Archetype derivation feedback loop: The derived archetype feeds back into the tier ceiling from Phase 2. A college with central-only IT (raw ceiling = Tier 2) but formal AI governance (Q7) gets upgraded to Building → Scaling, which adjusts the tier ceiling to Tier 3. The archetype — which synthesizes staffing and governance maturity — is a better judge of capacity than staffing alone.
Connection to assessment: The “Evaluate This Use Case →” button pre-selects the use case in the Use Case Selector and carries the archetype and institutional profile into the assessment. The user doesn’t re-answer capacity questions — that context travels with them. Users who already know their use case skip Step 0 entirely.

Risk Tier System

Tier Risk Level When It Applies Governance Requirements
Tier 1 Lower Risk Informational only. No decisions affecting access. No sensitive data. (e.g., FAQ chatbot) Score P1, P2, P6, P7, P8 minimum. Others recommended.
Tier 2 Medium Risk Touches advising or instructional core. Human reviews outputs. Accesses student records. (e.g., tutoring assistant) All 9 pillars scored. P2 and P6 must score ≥ 2.
Tier 3 High Risk Affects enrollment, financial aid, academic standing. Automated or semi-automated decisions. Sensitive data. (e.g., early alert system) All 9 pillars mandatory. P2 and P6 ≥ 2. Full governance review required.
Override mechanism: Each use case has a default risk tier. Users can override (e.g., a chatbot that accesses student records upgrades from Tier 1 to Tier 2). Lowering the tier requires a documented reason.

Eight No-Go Criteria

These are the hard stops. If any condition is true, the tool should not deploy until it’s resolved. Each has both a checklist detection path and a score-derived detection path.

NG-01: No clear educational purpose.
If you can’t articulate the educational purpose, don’t buy it.
Detection: P1 score = 1, OR screening indicates no educational purpose.
NG-02: Students are not training data.
Vendors that train models on student data are extracting value from the most vulnerable populations in higher education.
Detection: Explicit yes/no in no-go checklist.
NG-03: No human override for consequential decisions.
When AI makes decisions about financial aid, enrollment, or academic standing without human review, students lose recourse.
Detection: P3 score = 1 AND risk tier = High.
NG-04: Vendor blocks independent bias audits.
Vendors who block audits behind IP claims are choosing their property over your students’ civil rights.
Detection: Explicit yes/no in no-go checklist.
NG-05: No FERPA-compliant data sharing agreement.
This is a legal requirement, not a best practice.
Detection: Explicit yes/no in no-go checklist.
NG-06: No student disclosure of AI use.
Students have a right to know when AI influences decisions that affect them. Period.
Detection: P7 score = 1 OR explicit no-go question.
NG-07: Decisions without recourse are not governance.
If a student cannot contest an AI-influenced decision, you have automation, not governance.
Detection: P7 score = 1 OR explicit no-go question.
NG-08: Vendor lock-in undermines institutional sovereignty.
If you can’t leave without losing your students’ data or paying punitive switching costs, the vendor is governing you.
Detection: Explicit yes/no in no-go checklist.
Dual detection with proactive rendering: No-go criteria are triggered both by explicit checklist answers and by score-derived signals (e.g., P1=1 automatically flags NG-01). Score-derived flags are now applied at render time when the no-go checklist loads — auto-flagged items appear pre-set with an amber warning note explaining the trigger, and their radio buttons are disabled. This prevents a user from scoring a pillar poorly but not flagging the corresponding no-go, and makes the connection between pillar scores and hard stops visible and immediate.

Scoring & Interpretation

Per-Pillar Scoring

Each pillar is scored 1–3 using a rubric specific to that pillar:

Score Label Meaning
3 Good Governance requirements met. The tool and the institution’s practices align with pillar expectations.
2 Partial Basics met but gaps exist. Institution will need to compensate for vendor limitations.
1 Poor Critical governance gap. Remediation required before deployment (or no-go for high-risk tools).

Pattern Analysis

The profile screen interprets score patterns, not just individual scores:

Pattern Interpretation Action
Any pillar = 1 on a High-Risk tool Critical governance gap. For P2, P3, or P5 this is effectively a no-go. Do not deploy without documented mitigation plan.
P1 = 1 No clear educational purpose. No-go. If you can’t articulate the purpose, don’t buy it.
Multiple pillars = 2 Tool meets basics but has gaps the institution must compensate for. Common for tools not designed for CCs. Proceed with conditions. Document compensation plans.
All pillars ≥ 2 Solid baseline. Tool and governance aligned enough to proceed. Standard governance. Annual inventory. Monitor per schedule.
Strong P6 & P8, weak P4 & P7 Vendor is technically sound (good data, solid contracts) but product wasn’t designed with students as participants. Institution must build the student-facing layer: disclosure, contestability, opt-out, faculty config.
Strong P4 & P7, weak P6 Vendor markets well (student-friendly interface, good transparency) but hasn’t done data governance work. Investigate data practices deeply before proceeding.
P9 = 1 Not about the vendor or tool — it’s about institutional readiness. No governance structure. Build governance capacity first. Don’t add tools to an ungoverned environment.

Capability & Dependency Profile

A universal 4-question assessment asked after screening and before pillar scoring. Measures how dependent the institution would become on an AI tool — not as a warning, but as a governance input.

Key principle: Dependency is not inherently bad. Permanent infrastructure is valid. But the governance response should match the dependency level. A tool you could drop tomorrow needs different oversight than one woven into enrollment workflows.

Four Questions

# Question What It Measures Response Tiers (1 / 2 / 3)
D1 Disappearance Test Operational fragility — what happens if the tool vanishes tomorrow? 1 = Minor inconvenience / 2 = Significant disruption, workarounds exist / 3 = Service stops
D2 Capability Trajectory Skill direction — is the institution building capability or outsourcing it? 1 = Staff gaining skills / 2 = Stable, tool-assisted / 3 = Declining without tool
D3 Navigate-With vs. Navigate-For Agency — does the tool help staff do the work, or does it do the work? 1 = Staff drive, tool assists / 2 = Shared work / 3 = Tool drives, staff monitor
D4 Exit Design Reversibility — how hard is it to switch or stop? 1 = Easy switch / 2 = Possible with effort / 3 = Deeply embedded, costly exit

Scoring Bands

Score Range Profile Governance Response
4–5 Low Dependency No additional governance required beyond standard pillar-based assessment.
6–8 Medium Dependency Configuration review required. 6-month capability check. Alternative maintenance plan.
9–12 High Dependency Dependency acknowledgment. Vendor lock-in assessment. 12-month sunset evaluation. Capability impact tracking (Tier 3 only).

Design Principles

  1. Not gates. No dependency score blocks deployment. Scores trigger governance requirements, not stops.
  2. Permanent infrastructure is valid. A high dependency score for a mission-critical tool is expected, not alarming.
  3. Legitimize every answer. Tier 3 responses are honest, not wrong. The tool respects institutions that acknowledge deep integration.
  4. Patterns emerge through use. Dependency data across tools reveals institutional patterns (trending toward outsourcing vs. building capability).
  5. Dependency is not risk. Risk tier measures potential harm to students. Dependency measures institutional reliance. They interact but are distinct dimensions.

Interaction with Risk Tier

Dependency profile and risk tier are independent inputs that produce combined governance requirements:

  • High Dependency + High Risk (Tier 3): Triggers escalation — capability impact tracking added to governance requirements
  • High Dependency + declining capability: Escalation trigger in the roadmap
  • Low Dependency + any tier: No additional governance layer from dependency

Mechanism Detection

Risk tier should reflect the tool’s mechanism, not just its category. A campus life recommender and an FAQ chatbot both live in “Student-Facing Chatbot” (SS-04) — but one pattern-matches student behavior and the other serves static information. The governance requirements are fundamentally different.

Three questions detect mechanism types that reliably elevate risk regardless of what the tool is called. They run for every use case, after dependency profiling and before risk tier confirmation. Takes under 2 minutes.

Core principle: Instead of adding named use cases for every tool that might hide equity risk, the Navigator asks three mechanism questions that surface the risk regardless of category. SS-04 stays SS-04 — but if the chatbot pattern-matches, the Navigator catches it.

Three Mechanism Questions

ID Question What It Detects Escalation Triggers
MD-01 How does this tool decide what to show or recommend to each student? Behavioral pattern-matching. Tools that personalize outputs based on individual student data — behavioral history, profile characteristics, usage patterns, predicted preferences. This is the mechanism that turns a neutral chatbot into an equity risk. Options A–B: No escalation
C (profile-based): +1 tier. Force P2 ≥ 2, P7 ≥ 2.
D (behavioral): +1 tier. Force P2 ≥ 2, P7 ≥ 2. Equity flag.
E (unknown): +1 tier. Force P7 ≥ 2. Investigation required.
MD-02 Does this tool group, classify, or score individual students? Student classification & segmentation. Tools that create categories of students — risk scores, behavioral clusters, predicted outcomes. This mechanism hides inside early alert systems, registration tools, nudge campaigns, and advising platforms that “prioritize” outreach. Options A–B: No escalation
C (predictive scoring): +1 tier. Force P2 ≥ 2, P5 ≥ 2, P7 ≥ 2.
D (behavioral clustering): +1 tier. Force P2 ≥ 2, P5 ≥ 2, P7 ≥ 2. Equity flag.
E (unknown): +1 tier. Force P7 ≥ 2. Investigation required.
MD-03 Can this tool take actions or contact students without a human initiating it? Autonomous action & outreach. Tools that act on their own — sending messages, triggering workflows, escalating cases — without a human deciding “yes, do this for this student right now.” Turns a passive tool into an active agent in the student’s experience. Options A–B: No escalation
C (rule-based triggers): +1 tier (only if current Tier 1). Force P3 ≥ 2, P5 ≥ 2.
D (autonomous outreach): +1 tier. Force P3 ≥ 2, P5 ≥ 2, P7 ≥ 2.
E (cascading actions): +2 tiers (cap at 3). Force P3 ≥ 2, P5 ≥ 2, P7 ≥ 2. No-go flag for review.

Escalation Logic

Tier adjustments from mechanism detection are additive across all three questions but capped at Tier 3:

adjusted_tier = min(3, default_tier + MD01_escalation + MD02_escalation + MD03_escalation)
Example Default Tier Mechanism Flags Adjusted Tier
SS-04 Chatbot + profile-based personalization Tier 1 MD-01 C (+1) Tier 2
SS-04 Chatbot + behavioral matching + autonomous outreach Tier 1 MD-01 D (+1), MD-03 D (+1) Tier 3
SS-08 Career + predictive scoring Tier 1 MD-02 C (+1) Tier 2
SS-02 Early Alert + any mechanism flags Tier 3 Any Tier 3 (already max)

Forced Pillar Requirements

Mechanism flags force minimum pillar scores independent of tier. If a forced pillar scores below the minimum during pillar scoring, the Navigator surfaces a warning and carries the gap forward as a deployment blocker.

Mechanism Detected Forced Pillar Requirements
Profile-based personalization (MD-01 C) P2 ≥ 2, P7 ≥ 2
Behavioral pattern-matching (MD-01 D) P2 ≥ 2, P7 ≥ 2
Predictive scoring (MD-02 C) P2 ≥ 2, P5 ≥ 2, P7 ≥ 2
Behavioral clustering (MD-02 D) P2 ≥ 2, P5 ≥ 2, P7 ≥ 2
Autonomous outreach (MD-03 D) P3 ≥ 2, P5 ≥ 2, P7 ≥ 2
Cascading autonomous actions (MD-03 E) P3 ≥ 2, P5 ≥ 2, P7 ≥ 2
Unknown (any question) P7 ≥ 2
Equity screening flag: When MD-01 D (behavioral pattern-matching) or MD-02 D (behavioral clustering) is selected, an additional equity screening question is injected into the P2 (Equity) pillar scoring: “Has your institution tested whether this tool’s personalization or classification produces different outcomes for different student populations?” This forces the evaluator to confront the equity implications of the mechanism before scoring P2.

Design Principles

  1. Detection, not punishment. Every answer has a “what you’re looking for” description that legitimizes it. A tool that uses behavioral pattern-matching isn’t bad — it requires more governance.
  2. Transparent adjustments. The user sees exactly why the tier changed and which mechanism caused it. No black box.
  3. “Unknown” is the most important answer. If the governance team can’t answer how the tool personalizes, classifies, or acts autonomously, that’s the most critical finding. Treated as an investigation item, not a stop sign.
  4. Compounds with dependency profiling. A tool that is high-dependency AND uses behavioral pattern-matching is qualitatively different from either characteristic alone. Both dimensions are captured independently and combine in the governance profile.
  5. Solves the “Campus Life Recommender” problem. Instead of adding named use cases for every tool that might hide equity risk, three mechanism questions surface the risk regardless of category.

Assessment Logic

Routing Algorithm (Step 0)

The routing questionnaire processes 8 answers through five sequential phases to produce ranked use case recommendations.

Phase 1 — Signal Scoring
for each use_case (SS-01 through SS-10): score = 0
  Q1 selections: +3 pts to each mapped use case
  Q5 selections: +2 pts to each mapped use case
  cap: max 5 pts per use case (prevents broad-mapping use cases from dominating)

Phase 2 — Feasibility Gating
  Q3 data readiness → GREEN / YELLOW / RED flag per use case
  Q4 staffing → tier ceiling (dedicated=T3, central=T2, vendor-dep=T1)
  RED flag OR use_case_tier > ceiling → “Consider Later”

Phase 3 — Archetype Derivation
  archetype = f(Q4, Q7) with Q2 validation
  Q4 primary: dedicated → Scaling, central → Building, vendor/1-2 → Lightweight
  Q7 adjusts ±1: Building + formal governance → Scaling, etc.
  Q2 validates: flags unusual combos (e.g., <5K + Scaling)
  derived archetype OVERRIDES raw Q4 tier ceiling

Phase 4 — Contextual Overrides
  Q6 “already happening” → boost deployed tools to top
  Q6 “don’t know” → +2 to SS-04, add “start with AI inventory”
  Q8 motivation: +2 pts to motivation-matched use cases (sole location)

Phase 5 — Ranking & Output
  final_score = min(raw_signal, 5) + overrides
  sort: final_score desc → risk_tier asc → data_flag (G > Y > R)
  classify: GREEN + within ceiling = Recommended
           YELLOW or at ceiling = Possible (caveats)
           RED or above ceiling = Consider Later
  output: top 2–3 Recommended + “Why it fits” from Q1/Q5/Q8
  fallback: if no use case scores > 0, recommend SS-04 (Chatbot)

Full algorithm specification with complete mapping tables: see companion document Step 0: Student Services Use Case Routing Questionnaire.

Mechanism Detection Logic

Three questions (MD-01, MD-02, MD-03) produce a mechanism profile with tier adjustment and forced pillar requirements. Runs after dependency profiling, before risk tier confirmation.

// Escalation accumulator

// MD-01: Behavioral Pattern-Matching

if md01 = 'd': esc += 1, forced.P2 = 2, forced.P7 = 2, eqFlag = true

// MD-02: Student Classification

Screening Scoring

Each screening question answer has a logic property:

Logic Value Meaning Result Rule
go No concerns with this answer Does not trigger any flags
pause Concern flagged, not a deal-breaker 2+ pause answers = PAUSE result
nogo Hard stop condition Any nogo answer = NO-GO result
Screening formula:
if (nogo_count > 0) result = 'nogo'
else if (pause_count >= 2) result = 'pause'
else result = 'go'

Risk Tier Assignment

Each use case has a default_risk_tier (1, 2, or 3). Users can override. Risk tier determines:

  • Which pillars are mandatory vs. recommended
  • Minimum score requirements (P2 and P6 ≥ 2 for Tier 2+)
  • Roadmap complexity and pre-deployment requirements
  • RACI structure detail
  • Review cadence frequency

Pillar Scoring Rubrics

Each pillar has a scoring object with three keys ("3", "2", "1"). Rubric text is specific to each pillar. Users score the pillar holistically after reviewing the reference questions.

Design decision: Reference questions are displayed as guidance, not as individual scored items. The user reads 5–8 questions per pillar, then scores the pillar 1–3 as a whole. This is faster (54 questions would take too long to score individually) and produces a more actionable output (pillar-level scores map directly to remediation actions).

Pattern Matching Algorithm

After scoring, the profile runs pattern analysis against the user’s scores. Logic:

for each pattern in SCORE_PATTERNS:
  if pattern = "Any pillar = 1" AND riskTier = 3:
    check each pillar; if any = 1, match
  if pattern = "P1 = 1": check P1 score
  if pattern = "P9 = 1": check P9 score
  if pattern = "Multiple pillars = 2": count pillars with score 2; if >= 3, match
  if pattern = "All pillars >= 2": check all scored pillars >= 2 AND scoredCount >= 5
  if pattern = "Strong P6 & P8, weak P4 & P7": check P6>=3 AND P8>=3 AND (P4<=1 OR P7<=1)
  if pattern = "Strong P4 & P7, weak P6": check P4>=3 AND P7>=3 AND P6<=1

matched patterns display with interpretation + action
// Dependency-aware pattern (added by Section F):
if dependencyProfile = 'high' AND capabilityTrajectory = 3 (declining):
  add escalation trigger: "High dependency with declining institutional capability"

The California Toggle

A checkbox in the nav bar labeled “CA”. When enabled:

  • Pillar headers show HUMANS letter mapping (e.g., P3 shows “H” badge)
  • Profile pillar table includes HUMANS column
  • Roadmap adds HUMANS Alignment section with letter-by-letter mapping
  • Roadmap notes the HUMANS gap (P9 / Criterion 4 not covered)

When disabled: none of the above appears. The framework is complete without it.

Technical Notes

Aspect Details
Architecture Single-file HTML. No build step. No framework. All JavaScript inline. Content JSON inlined with fallback external fetch.
Dependencies Zero runtime dependencies. Google Fonts loaded for display (Plus Jakarta Sans + Inter) with system fallback.
State Management Plain JavaScript object (state). Tracks: entry path, archetype, use case, screening answers/result/flags, dependency profile (D1–D4 answers + total score + profile level), risk tier (+ override flag/reason), pillar scores/notes, no-go answers/flags, California toggle, assessment date, tool name, institution name, customWorstCase (SS-XX harm scenario text), routingAnswers (Q1–Q8 answers object), routingResults (computed recommendations), currentRoutingQ (progress index), mechanismProfile (MD-01/02/03 answers + computed tierAdjustment, adjustedTier, forcedPillars, equityFlag, unknownFlags). Full state is serializable to JSON for export/import/URL sharing.
Persistence Three mechanisms: (1) localStorage — quick save/load within the same browser. (2) JSON export/import — serializes full state to a downloadable .json file; import via file picker validates the presence of useCase or pillarScores before merging into state. (3) Shareable URL — base64-encodes state into a ?state= URL parameter (capped at 8,000 chars; falls back to export if too large). On init, the app checks for the state URL param, decodes and loads the assessment, navigates to profile, then cleans the URL with replaceState. No backend, no tracking, no analytics.
Navigation View-based SPA. Hash routing (#landing, #use-case, #dependency/{use-case-id}, etc.). Browser back/forward supported via popstate. Nav bar includes: logo, About, Import (triggers file picker), and CA toggle.
Print CSS @media print rules. Hides nav, buttons. Shows profile and roadmap views for export.
Hosting Any static server. GitHub Pages, Netlify, Vercel, or a simple web server. No server-side logic required.
Privacy No data collection. No cookies. No tracking. No external API calls. Assessment results stay in the browser.
Accessibility Keyboard navigable. Semantic HTML. Color contrast meets WCAG 2.1 AA on critical elements. Screen reader compatible (ARIA labels on interactive elements).
Responsive Mobile-first breakpoint at 768px. Assessment works one-question-per-screen on mobile. Dashboard stacks vertically. Score options stack to single column.

About This Document
This concept document describes the design rationale, framework architecture, user journey, and content architecture of the AI Governance Navigator v2 — an interactive governance tool for Student Services AI use cases at community colleges. It captures the what, why, and how for team alignment.

Date: February 22, 2026 (updated February 25, 2026)
Team: Amin, Mimi, Adam
Source: AQL Ethical AI Framework (4 criteria, 9 pillars, 10 use cases, 8 no-go criteria, 3 archetypes), CCCCO HUMANS Framework, global AI governance research (224 frameworks across 12 domains)
Commissioned by: College Futures Foundation
Companion deliverable: AI Governance Toolkit PDF (complete reference with all templates, playbooks, and measurement guidance)

Feb 25 update: Documented four UX improvements implemented in the prototype: (1) progressive disclosure for pillar scoring via accordion toggle, (2) proactive auto-flagging of no-go criteria from pillar scores at render time, (3) JSON export/import and shareable URL for assessment persistence, (4) forced worst-case harm scenario for SS-XX custom use cases. Also documented Step 0: Routing Questionnaire — 8-question intake with 5-phase scoring algorithm that routes institutions to recommended use cases (see companion spec document). Also documented Mechanism Detection Screen — 3 questions (behavioral pattern-matching, student classification, autonomous action) with additive tier escalation, forced pillar minimums, and equity screening flags (see companion spec document).
