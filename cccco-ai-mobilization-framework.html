The AI Mobilization Framework is the operational blueprint for integrating AI across the largest higher education system in the United States. It implements Vision 2030 Action 12 under Strategic Direction 3: Generative AI and the Future of Learning.

Central proposition: The CCC system requires “immediate strategic mobilization” at “startup velocity”—compressing traditional higher education timelines from years to months—while maintaining educational excellence and equity commitments.

Four Pillars of Mobilization

Pillar 1: Culture Cultivation

Cultivating a culture that engages existing talent and recruits innovators within a belief system that actualizes Vision 2030. Sustainable transformation requires cultural change management alongside technological advancement, ensuring AI integration serves educational mission rather than replacing it.

Pillar 2: Innovation Behaviors

Modeling behaviors that facilitate the speed and mindset of positive change across seven focus areas and within numerous communities of practice. Addresses the need for an innovation mindset that can operate at speeds uncommon in higher education bureaucracies.

Pillar 3: Technical Infrastructure & Partnerships

Identifying, demonstrating, adopting, and scaling technical infrastructure and partnerships that support rapid adoption. Includes the Common Cloud Data Platform ($10M initial + $12M scaling), industry MOUs (Google, NVIDIA, Microsoft, IBM, Intel, OpenAI, Adobe), and the Digital Center for Innovation, Transformation and Equity.

Pillar 4: Empowered Communities of Champions

Deploying distributed leadership composed of small, empowered communities of champions across all constituency groups. The operational heart of the framework: a multi-tiered role structure (Fellows, ChangeMakers, Ambassadors, Scholars, Innovators) distributed across regions and colleges. ChangeMakers across 116 colleges “simulate multiple local innovation nodes that operate simultaneously, generating rapid system-wide learning through parallel experimentation.”

HUMANS Ethical Framework

The HUMANS framework provides binding ethical guardrails for all AI adoption across the system. All AI solutions must adhere to these principles.

H
Human-Centered
Include humans in design and testing. Preserve faculty and student agency. AI complements—not replaces—people.
U
Universal Support
Opt-out rights where appropriate. Equitable access to tools and training. Right to human support alternatives.
M
Managed Privacy
User agency over personal data collection. Control over how data is collected, stored, and used.
A
Algorithmic Discrimination
Swift action to remedy discriminatory tools. Active monitoring and audit mechanisms. Bias-free systems.
N
Notice & Explanation
Sufficient notification about automated systems, impact on users, and contribution to outcomes.
S
Safety & Security
Protection from unsafe or ineffective tools. AI systems meet security standards.

AI Workplan: Six Activities

The Vision 2030 Artificial Intelligence Workplan (July 2025) consists of six activities driving Outcomes 2, 4, 5, and 6:

Activity 1: AI Infrastructure & Data Governance

Common Cloud Data Platform ($10M initial + $12M scaling) for centralized data management, real-time analytics, student progress tracking, program mapping, and automated credit transfers. DGAW standardizes data practices. Tools include Program Pathways Mapper, eTranscripts, CSU Transfer Planner, and a Chancellor's LLM-Dashboard.

Activity 2: Student Support

AI-driven tutoring, 24/7 AI chatbots for personalized student support, fraud detection, and Credit for Prior Learning automation. Uses the Common Cloud Data Platform to enhance proactive support and guidance.

Activity 3: Professional Development

AI Fellows and Advisors creating a bidirectional workflow connecting local and statewide initiatives. Targeted workshops, discipline-specific guides, and assessment tools. Systemwide training via AI Council and Vision Resource Center.

Activity 4: Workforce Development

Expanding workforce readiness by making GenAI tools and training accessible to students and external partners including small businesses. Built on industry MOUs with Google, NVIDIA, Microsoft, IBM, Intel, and OpenAI.

Activity 5: Teaching & Learning

AI integration across disciplines—from nursing and automotive technology to business and liberal arts. Developing student-facing AI curricula. AI literacy not confined to STEM.

Activity 6: AI Policy & AI Literacy

Expanding AI literacy and ethical AI practices. AI literacy as a foundational learning outcome for all subjects. Standardizing ethical guidelines aligned with HUMANS framework.

HUMANS ↔ Nine Pillars Alignment

The CCCCO HUMANS framework and AQL’s nine governance pillars are complementary. This table maps the alignment:

HUMANS Principle AQL Pillar(s) Relationship
H Human-Centered Approach P3: Human Authority & Decision Accountability
P4: Student and Faculty Agency		 HUMANS “H” establishes the principle; P3 operationalizes it with governance structures ensuring humans — not algorithms — make consequential decisions. P4 extends it to faculty pedagogical authority and student participation. AB 2370 provides legislative floor.
U Universal Support P2: Equity & Differential Impact
P4: Student and Faculty Agency		 Opt-out rights and equitable access align with the Equity pillar’s differential impact analysis. P4 extends “universal” to faculty agency (including 70%+ adjunct workforce) and adult learner autonomy.
M Managed Privacy Controls P6: Data Stewardship & System Integrity User agency over data aligns directly. P6 extends to data lifecycle requirements, institutional data sovereignty, CCCCO DGAW alignment, and incident response protocols.
A Algorithmic Discrimination P2: Equity & Differential Impact
P5: Risk Proportionality & Harm Mitigation		 Direct alignment. HUMANS requires remediation; P2 adds disaggregated outcome measurement, bias audits, and differential impact analysis. P5 adds risk-proportionate governance ensuring high-impact AI receives rigorous oversight.
N Notice & Explanation P7: Transparency & Contestability Direct alignment. P7 adds classroom-level disclosure, public-facing governance documentation, meaningful explanation of AI outputs, and the right to contest AI-influenced decisions through accessible processes.
S Safety & Security P8: Vendor Accountability & Institutional Sovereignty
P9: Institutional Capacity & Continuous Governance		 Safety aligns with vendor accountability (P8: evaluation checklists, audit rights, contract addenda) and institutional capacity (P9: governance reporting, tiered implementation, progressive maturity). P5 (Risk Proportionality) also contributes through incident escalation and harm mitigation protocols.
Key finding: HUMANS and the nine pillars are structurally compatible. HUMANS sets system-wide ethical principles; the nine pillars operationalize them at the institutional governance level. They complement rather than compete.
