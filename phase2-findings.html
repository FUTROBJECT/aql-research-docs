Executive Summary

Phase 2 searched 12 targeted domains to fill gaps identified in Phase 1 and build the evidence base for network governance. Seven key findings emerged:

1. The CC system already has a network governance model.

The CCCCO AI Mobilization Framework (2025) describes a polycentric structure with AI Fellows (13), ChangeMakers (110), regional consortia, and the HUMANS ethical framework. The Network Governance section of our framework should reference and build on this—not design from scratch.

2. No CC system anywhere has a comprehensive AI governance framework.

Most have piecemeal guidance focused on academic integrity. California has the opportunity to be the first system with an integrated, equity-centered, theoretically grounded AI governance model.

3. Multiple state systems have produced governance models worth studying.

SUNY (federated strategy), Georgia (board mandate), Tennessee (legislative mandate), VCCS (federated service), Oklahoma (collaborative committee), and CUNY (equity-centered shared governance) each represent a distinct archetype.

4. Faculty governance is activating through three channels.

Academic senates (ASCCC), unions (AFT/NEA/CFA/CFT), and advocacy organizations (FACCC/AAUP) are all simultaneously engaging on AI. AI is becoming a mandatory bargaining subject.

5. Critical gaps remain unfilled.

No adjunct-specific AI governance, no non-credit/ESL AI governance, no transfer student AI policy portability, no CC student AI rights framework, no accessibility-specific AI governance.

6. Network governance theory provides strong but untapped foundations.

Provan & Kenis, Ansell & Gash, Ostrom, Emerson/Nabatchi/Balogh, and Feiock provide robust theoretical grounding—but no published work applies these to educational AI governance. This is an original contribution opportunity.

7. Cooperative procurement infrastructure exists but lacks AI criteria.

HECVAT 4 added AI questions, 1EdTech has a GenAI Data Rubric, Digital Promise offers independent certification, FoundationCCC/CollegeBuys operates system-wide—but no CC procurement cooperative has AI-specific evaluation criteria yet.

Confirmed Gaps Still Remaining

Even after Phase 2, no authoritative framework exists for these areas:

1. CC student-specific AI rights
No framework addresses the unique circumstances of community college students—adult learners, part-time, working, transferring between institutions.
2. Adjunct-specific AI governance
No framework addresses AI governance for the approximately 70% of CC instruction delivered by part-time faculty.
3. Non-credit/ESL AI governance
No framework governs AI in WIOA Title II programs, ESL, adult basic education, or non-credit workforce training.
4. Transfer student AI policy portability
No framework harmonizes AI policies across institutions for transferring students.
5. AI competency articulation
No articulation agreements cover AI skills or competencies.
6. Equity-centered AI procurement
No procurement framework centers equity for institutions serving historically underserved populations.
7. AI in assessment governance
No framework governs AI use in CASAS, TABE, or other standardized assessments for adult learners.
8. CC-specific AI accessibility
No guidance addresses the 15% vs. 35% disability disclosure rate gap between 2-year and 4-year institutions.
9. Collective bargaining AI contract language
Template language is under development (AFT, NEA, EI) but no negotiated provisions are publicly available.
10. Inter-system coordination
No framework addresses CCC-CSU-UC alignment on AI governance despite shared transfer pathways.
The gaps: Areas where the CC Ethical AI Framework could make an original contribution that no other system has made.
About This Document: This document presents findings from Phase 2 research for the Ethical AI Framework for California Community Colleges. Phase 2 searched 12 additional domains (Domains 13–24) to fill gaps identified in Phase 1 and build the evidence base for network governance. The research identified approximately 351 candidates across CC system governance, state system models, higher education consortia, faculty networks, student rights, transfer and credentials, accessibility, adjunct faculty, non-credit and adult education, network governance theory, recent 2025–2026 frameworks, and cooperative procurement. Phase 1 covered 210+ frameworks across 12 domains focused on global AI governance, regional frameworks, sector-specific standards, civil society, indigenous data sovereignty, and corporate governance.
