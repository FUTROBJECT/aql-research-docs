Executive Narrative

I tried to cast a large net with this discovery effort, and searched 12 domains of AI governance to identify frameworks relevant to College Futures' Ethical AI Framework for California community colleges. The research reveals a quickly growing global landscape. With over 200 frameworks identified and significant possible gaps:

Community colleges remain largely invisible in authoritative AI governance frameworks.

The Global Picture

AI governance has reached an inflection point. The 2024-2025 period saw an unprecedented wave of binding legislation: Peru became among the first in Latin America to enact a general AI law (Law 31814, July 2023),[9] South Korea passed its AI Basic Act in December 2024,[11] Japan enacted the Act on Promotion of Research and Development of AI-Related Technologies in May 2025,[10][12] Taiwan passed its AI Basic Act in December 2025,[13][14] and Brazil's Marco Legal advanced through Congress. The EU AI Act entered into force on August 1, 2024, with obligations phased in through 2027,[8] triggering ripple effects globally as regulators looked to Brussels for precedent.

Regional coordination has accelerated. Africa adopted both the AU Continental AI Strategy[4] and the Africa Declaration on AI (signed in Kigali), establishing the Africa AI Council through Smart Africa[18] with ambitious financing targets, though fund capitalization and governance structures remain under development.[17] Latin America built on the 2023 Santiago Declaration with the Montevideo Roadmap, creating a forum for ongoing ministerial coordination.[16] ASEAN expanded its 2024 AI governance guide with GenAI-specific provisions in January 2025.[3][15]

What's Missing: The Education Gap

Despite this activity, education remains peripheral in global AI governance. While the EU AI Act does classify education as a high-risk domain under Annex III (e.g., AI used for exam scoring or determining access to education),[8] it provides minimal implementation guidance for educational institutions. National strategies acknowledge AI's educational potential but rarely provide operational frameworks. The frameworks that do address education—UNESCO's competency frameworks, TeachAI's principles, EDUCAUSE's resources—focus primarily on K-12 or four-year institutions.

Community colleges are essentially absent. Of the 224 frameworks reviewed, our analysis identified only TWO that directly address community colleges: the Academic Senate for California Community Colleges (ASCCC) policy framework[19] and the Achieving the Dream network's AI Task Force report.[1] This is an initial analysis—the claim of exclusivity cannot be conclusively verified without exhaustive review of all 224 frameworks—but if confirmed it is a remarkable oversight given that community colleges serve approximately 43.7% of all US undergraduates (2020-21),[20] disproportionately serve historically marginalized populations, and are the primary pipeline for workforce training that AI is transforming.

Potential Bright Spots for Adaptation

Several frameworks, while not education-specific, seem to offer models directly adaptable to the CC context:

The AI Handbook for Local Government (University of Michigan) was designed explicitly for resource-constrained public institutions without dedicated compliance teams—a description that fits most community colleges precisely.
IEEE P7004 is the only international standards project specifically addressing child and student data governance.[6] Currently an active draft (not yet a published standard), it is essential reading for any educational AI framework.
The NAIC Model Bulletin on AI for insurers provides a remarkably detailed governance template—requiring written AI programs, cross-functional accountability structures, and documentation standards—that translates well to institutional AI policy despite its insurance focus.[2]
OMB M-25-21, the current federal AI policy, explicitly lists "access to education" as a "high-impact AI use" alongside healthcare, housing, and employment.[5] This creates potential compliance implications for federally-funded institutions.
The Ethical Framework for AI in Education (Institute for Ethical AI in Education, University of Buckingham) is the only procurement-focused framework in the inventory. Its nine requirements and vendor evaluation checklist provide an immediately actionable tool for CC technology selection committees. Adopted by Khan Academy and licensed CC BY-NC 4.0 for institutional adaptation.

The Frontier: Agentic AI

Maybe the most significant for forward-looking governance is the emergence of frameworks for agentic AI: systems that can autonomously pursue goals, make decisions, and take actions. Singapore's CSA published a draft addendum on securing agentic AI systems for public consultation in October 2025,[21] and IMDA launched the Model AI Governance Framework for Agentic AI in January 2026[22]—together representing the first major government attempts to govern these systems. The newly-formed Agentic AI Foundation under the Linux Foundation, with backing from Anthropic, OpenAI, and others, is establishing infrastructure standards through the Model Context Protocol.[23]

This matters for community colleges because agentic AI is coming to education rapidly. AI tutoring agents, advising chatbots, and automated student support systems are no longer speculative—they're being deployed. Yet no framework addresses agentic AI in educational contexts. CC institutions adopting these tools today are essentially governing in a vacuum.

Indigenous Data Sovereignty: A Model for Collective Consent

The Indigenous data sovereignty movement offers valuable principles for CC's equity-centered mission. The CARE Principles (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics), the Canadian OCAP framework, and New Zealand's Te Mana Raraunga challenge the individual consent model that dominates AI governance. They argue that data about communities should benefit those communities and be governed collectively.

For community colleges serving place-based, historically marginalized communities, this framing resonates. A student's educational data isn't just theirs individually—it reflects and affects their communities. UNESCO's new Guidelines on Indigenous Data Sovereignty in AI specifically apply these principles to AI development, offering a model for culturally-responsive educational AI governance.

Recommendations

Based on this discovery, possible recommendations for College Futures:

1 Prioritize extraction and analysis of the ASCCC framework,[19] Achieving the Dream report,[1] IEEE P7004,[6] and the AI Handbook for Local Government as directly applicable resources.
2 Develop CC-specific guidance filling the identified gaps—particularly for adult learners, workforce education, adjunct faculty considerations, and non-credit programs that other frameworks ignore.
3 Prepare for agentic AI by adapting Singapore's Agentic AI governance frameworks[21][22] to educational contexts before tutoring and advising agents proliferate.
4 Integrate Indigenous data sovereignty principles through Local Contexts TK Labels and CARE frameworks, recognizing community colleges' unique community-serving mission.
5 Monitor pending legislation including the federal AI Civil Rights Act (which explicitly covers education) and Brazil's Marco Legal (as a model for risk-based regulation in resource-constrained contexts).
The global AI governance landscape offers a lot of possible resources, but community colleges must adapt these frameworks rather than adopt them directly. This discovery phase provides the raw material; the further analysis and synthesis phase will need translate it to CC realities.

Domain Summaries

1. Africa (15 frameworks)
The AU Continental AI Strategy (2024)[4] and the Africa Declaration on AI (2025) establish pan-African governance. Nigeria, Kenya, Rwanda, Egypt, and South Africa lead national efforts. The Africa AI Council launched via Smart Africa in November 2025.[18] Key insight: emphasis on data sovereignty and local computing capacity aligns with CC equity concerns.
2. Asia-Pacific (26 frameworks)
Most dynamic regulatory region. Japan's AI promotion act (2025),[10][12] South Korea AI Basic Act (2024),[11] Taiwan AI Basic Act (2025)[13][14] represent binding legislation wave. ASEAN's voluntary guide covers 10 nations.[3] Singapore's Agentic AI governance frameworks are globally significant.[21][22] China's labeling requirements and India's light-touch approach offer contrasting models.
3. Latin America (22 frameworks)
Santiago (2023) and Montevideo (2024) Declarations create regional coordination.[16] Peru enacted Law 31814 (2023), among the first general AI laws in the region.[9] Brazil's Marco Legal most advanced pending bill. Chile first to complete UNESCO readiness assessment. Strong equity and vulnerable populations focus aligns with CC mission.
4. Industry Standards (22 frameworks)
ISO/IEC 42001 certification accelerating. ISO 23894 (risk) and 38507 (governance) complement it. IEEE P7004 is the only standards project addressing student data governance (currently in development).[6] NIST GenAI Profile (AI 600-1) addresses LLM risks. AI Verify offers open-source testing capability.
5. Financial Services (19 frameworks)
Most operationalized sector governance. MAS Veritas provides "executable ethics" model. EU AI Act classifies credit scoring as high-risk.[8] CFPB requires specific adverse action reasons. NAIC Model Bulletin offers adaptable governance template.[2]
6. Healthcare (17 frameworks)
High-stakes parallel to education decisions. WHO LMM guidance addresses GenAI. FDA GMLP harmonizes US/Canada/UK. CHAI assurance framework with "model cards" offers transparency model. NAM Code of Conduct provides six commitments framework.
7. Defense/Government (20 frameworks)
OMB M-25-21 current federal policy—explicitly mentions education.[5] GAO framework provides audit methodology. UK AI Playbook offers 10 principles. AI Handbook for Local Government directly addresses resource-constrained institutions.
8. Civil Society & Human Rights (17 frameworks)
Freedom Online Coalition (2025) explicitly addresses education. AI Civil Rights Act would cover educational decisions. Amnesty toolkit enables community oversight. AI Now 2025 report offers critical industry analysis.
9. Indigenous Data Sovereignty (15 frameworks)
CARE Principles foundational. OCAP (Canada) and Maiam nayri Wingara (Australia) establish national frameworks. UNESCO Indigenous AI Guidelines bridge data sovereignty and AI. Local Contexts TK Labels offer practical implementation tool.
10. Frontier AI (16 frameworks)
Seoul Summit commitments from 20 organizations. International AI Safety Institutes network coordinates government efforts. OpenAI agentic practices paper addresses autonomous systems. Agentic AI Foundation/MCP establishing infrastructure standard.[23]
11. Corporate Governance (13 frameworks)
Microsoft, Google, IBM publish transparency reports. ISACA COBIT adapts IT governance for AI. Major vendors shape tools CC uses—understanding their governance informs vendor evaluation.
12. Education-Adjacent (22 frameworks)
EDUCAUSE provides most comprehensive higher ed resources. ASCCC framework directly addresses California CCs.[19] Achieving the Dream report reflects CC network perspectives.[1] Advance CTE covers workforce programs. Accreditors (SACSCOC, C-RAC, HLC) developing AI guidance with compliance implications.

Confirmed Gaps

No authoritative frameworks exist for:
  • Community college-specific AI governance (partially addressed by ASCCC and ATD, but no comprehensive CC governance standard exists)
  • Adult learner/non-traditional student AI contexts
  • Dual enrollment AI governance (K-12/higher ed intersection)
  • Non-credit/continuing education AI
  • Adjunct/contingent faculty AI implications
  • Student-facing agentic AI (tutoring agents, advising bots)
  • Transfer student AI (articulation, credit evaluation)
  • Disability/accessibility in AI (Alabama template and ASCCC include accessibility provisions, but no dedicated AI accessibility governance framework exists)
  • Small organization implementation pathways (the AI Handbook for Local Government partially addresses resource-constrained contexts, but no education-specific guidance exists)
